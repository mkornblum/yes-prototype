The New York Times Profiles YES!

Students, Families and Schools say YES!
by Helen Selway
Published: December 11, 2016

Two years ago, Quinn Dearborn started her junior year of high school with the usual mix of excitement and nervousness about what role the next couple of years would play in shaping her future, especially as the pressure of “what’s next” gained steam. Because community service credits are a big part of the experience of MLK High, she was empowered with a opportunity through her school’s participation in the national YES! (Youth Entrepreneurs for Social !mpact) program to channel her raw passion into real results.

With an instructional strategy and toolkit that students, families or schools can utilize for making social impact, YES! participants are able to meet academic standards by participating in an organized service project of their own making that addresses community needs and fosters civic responsibility. YES! leverages existing incentive structures, existing requirements and resources to make it happen.

“It made me feel confident about making a difference with what I was already interested in and helped me turn my ideas it into action,” Dearborn says. “I loved spending elective class time on something I can see now helped me in my longer-term career”. Through the program, she took her love of bass music, soundsystems and automotives to start a service for repurposing sound systems parts called GoBoom. GoBoom educates and employs local youth in refurbishing, recycling and reselling audio parts for cars - which has turned into a profitable ongoing business.

YES! initially launched in the Seattle area, then grew city by city across the US to help build upon existing community service requirements to help get students to move beyond the initial idea phase into a real-life experience of implementing a service or product. “We saw lots of opportunities out there for supporting youth who are already interested in learning about social entrepreneurship, but we wanted to increase the pool that are able to participate and learn from such an experience,” said Zach Smith, co-founder of the program.

Integrating with schools was the missing link that YES! was able to innovate on. Through an extensive research phase, they found many young people with varying levels of exposure to entrepreneurial experiences still found it challenging to balance the demands of school. “Connecting service learning to your community through your school were the dots that we saw that needed to be connected”, Smith says.

“The refreshing part of how YES! changed the conversation, is that schools never really used to ask kids “What are you passionate about? How will you use that to create change?” says Willie Mack, principal of MLK, “Having students leverage their own personal experience and passion is key to success.”

YES! also uses the funds from the purchases of the extended school curriculum to host a national pitch event where organizations created by YES! students can compete for funding. “It’s a great way to build momentum, get extra exposure and excitement around the amazing ideas that this generation is making happen” says Mack.
